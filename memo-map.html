process

1 | ideation + research

what app idea do we have?

try photos and friends as pins on map

Tasked with "creating a mobile app that addresses an unment need", we turned towards our own interests since we wanted an idea we were alll passionate about. Between traveling, exploring, photos, and especially food, we explored the current market of recommendations, travel content, locations, and photos and how they were being used in different apps.

fig. screenshots of find my friends, google maps, photo app used as inspiration

In an initial user research survey on our target demographic of 17 young adults:

  • 12/17 rank friends and family most when it comes to recommendations
  • 15/17 use their camera roll to keep track of places visited
  • 11/17 share travel content the most on social media
  • 13/17 use BeReal and like the app for the following reasons
    • "fun way to interact and keep in touch with friends everyday"
    • "app creativity"
    • "low effort and low commitment"
    • "ability to look back on past BeReals as a daily journal"

With additional secondary research, we identified specific pain points:

  • craving for human connection and seeing what your friends are up to
  • overthinking social media posts and forgetting to live in the moment
  • high effort required when documenting travel experiences
  • overload of recommendations when looking for places to visit

fig. user research survey results

Based on these findings and our ideas, we defined 3 main features:

  1. adding a photo/memory/pin
  2. viewing you and your friends map
  3. answering the daily prompt and viewing friends' answers

2 | wireframing

how do we fit 3 these features into an intuitive, unique app design? with figma of course :)

Started off with low-fi wireframes â fidelity wise are high content, medium depth + interaction, low visual + breadth.

I focused on User Flow 2 â searching and viewing your friends map. While designing, we faced many design opportunities and possibilites where we quickly created iterations and left the decisions to user testing.

lo-fi wireframes

flow 1: adding a photo to your map

flow 2: searching and viewing you and your friends map

user testing insights

flow 1: adding a photo to your map

  • most users preferred a camera tab for easier access and similarity to other social media design pattern
  • preferred pressing the shutter button once to take both photos as opposed to twice
  • adjusting a location felt intuitive

flow 2: searching and viewing you and your friends map

  • every user preferred toggle while the single icon was confusing to identify the maps

flow 3: answering the daily prompt and viewing friends' answers

  • pop-up felt too similar to Apple's default pop-up buttons for error messages or scams --> turned this into a button
  • left/right recents swiping gesture not intuitive --> decided to switch to grid format
  • too many clicks to answer the prompt --> added available photos on the map when selecting
  • users liked the uniqueness of map view compared to a typical feed view --> decided to keep both as options for the user

I took the lead on wireframing moving forward and took these findings into consideration for iterating on mid-fi wireframes â high content, medium depth + interaction + visual, low breadth.

3 | prototyping + api + database

turn wireframes interactive and ensure proof of concept for development

From our hi-fi wireframes, I created an interative prototype, building upon the breath and depth into an fully encompassing user experience. This included screens for user profiles and maintaining friends. I conducted 2 of 4 user tests with the prototype. After giving users a brief basis of the app, we asked them to think aloud as a usability test.

insert gif of prototype here

As for our API, we researched and compared both Apple's MapKit and Google Maps API. After configuring and testing both APIs, we decided to use Apple's MapKit for its streamlined integration with XCode and more flexible ability to drop pins. This was to ensure we could implement to core functionalities planned for the app.

In order to store data and images, we designed a Firestore NoSQL database structure through data mapping our wireframes. We then set up the corresponding database inside the Firebase system.

3 | developing version 1

code an mvp (harder than we thought)

For this first version, we determined A-level features to tackle first

  • creating a memory with camera (flow 1)
  • viewing memories as pins on the map (flow 2)
  • adding friends

While the features sounded simple enough, they proved to be way more complex in practice. We began to face continous challenges in development. Between XCode setup across different devices, minimal Swift knowledge/experience, and consistent merge conflicts with Git, we faced a steep learning curve in the two weeks we had to develop an MVP.

I began the development process with the dual style camera functionality. This took me about 18 hours for a working camera with proper access and threading configurations. Slightly buggy but working, I was able to capure both front and back images together successfully. After completing the camera to an MVP level, I focused on the navigation and framework of the entire app â coding different modular front-end pieces and filling gaps in where needed.

My two team members took on Firebase and MapKit which had challenges with custom pins/annotations of MapKit and storing images in Firebase.

By the end of this two week sprint, we built most of our features with a functioning front-end, but no back-end and styling.

4 | user testing version 1

get feedback on our (rough) mvp

We conducted 6 sessions with target users asked think aloud as they freely navigated the app. As expected, most of our feebdack centered around UI elements which didn't quite match our design prototype due to time and/or technical constraints.

feedback action item
icons are too small and hard to see against the complex map background increase size of icons and mute colors in the map
search bar more bothersome than helpful â had to use search again and not able to hit "return" to execute search include "return" button to search
camera shutter button too small and slow response; simple and intutive due to similarity to BeReal increase size of button and increase efficiency/feedback of camera
friend requests discoverability was high and experience was intuitive none
map as a whole needed UI improvements with size of the search bar, look of the pins, and distracting labels of locations of interest overall UI improvements to the map, closer to our designs and working around MapKit constraints

As expected, we received lots of feedback on the UI, hindering the user experience and emphasizing the importance of a strong UI. We revised our development with the action items above, addressing issues our participants faced.
