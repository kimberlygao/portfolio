01 Research + Personas

We started our design process by defining key stakeholders in the rental space:

  • Customer –– Landlord
  • Business Ownder –– Head of Property Management Company
  • Service Provider –– Leasing Agent

We began researching, creating personas, and developing intial scenarios for all 3 stakeholders. Due time constraints of 36 hours, we conducted secondary research and later confirmed our designs through primary research.

Landlord Insights

Learnings

  • being a landlord is risky
  • it's difficult to find the right tenants
  • a large percentage of landlords often use property management companies

Key Priorities

  • easy, efficient communication with renters and maintenance
  • easy screening

Takeaway –– painpoints are currently addressed by solutions that exist in the market, not sure what value we'd be able to add here.

Leasing Agent Insights

Learnings

  • leasing agents constantly on the move but still need to handle client communication
  • responsible for ensuring all units assigned to them are occupied
  • a large percentage of landlords often use property management companies

Key Priorities

  • easy communication with tenants
  • quick and efficient scheduling with prospective renters
  • maintaining an organized view of tasks and properties

Takeaway –– felt like a user that would heavily benefit from a responsive design solution due to the nature of their job.

Property Management Head Insights

Learnings

  • renting an apartment today is a fragmented experience – searching, scheduling, viewing
  • scheduling is a key issue due to a number of changed/cancelled appointments

Key Priorities

  • creating a "super all in one application" that consolidates these fragmented experiences into one view
  • matching the right people to properties

Takeaway –– interesting to think about the rental expereince from the perspective of the Zillow CEO and the point around fragmented nature of rental expereinces today was an element we decided that we were interested in exploring further.

Revised Stakeholders

From our intial research process, we discovered a lot of overlap and role sharing for the personas we initially investigated. We also realized that the rental perspective (which we did not pursue at first) was crucial due to the large interaction with the leasing agent.

We decided to pivot and focus on the Leasing Agent and Renter as our stakeholders. We were especially interested in the Leasing Agent perspective because we felt like they would benefit the most from a responsive design solution.

03 Refinement –– Speed Dating and User Interviews

Scenario, Storyboard, & Sketches Speed Dating

We conducted two speed dating sessions for our different ideas and gathered some useful insights.

Ideas

  • refining log-in process for privacy and security
  • a timeline or notfication feature
  • communication methods within the platform

Feedback

  • looking into existing listing platforms for design patterns
  • introduce something different that makes us competitive with other existing platforms
Leasing Agent User Interview

Before diving into lo-fi prototypes, we were able to conduct a user interview with a local leasing agent for more insight. We asked about their current set of tasks (leases, tours, maintenance, scheduling, and move-in) and challenges they're currently facing. This was very helpful for guidance on what to include in our prototypes.

Key Insights

  • focus on desktop and mobile
  • need a consolidated portal for easy task management
  • need an easy way to show properies and listings
  • scheduling is a major pain point
  • move in details need an easy way to be sent out

We discovered they use desktop and mobile devices most frequently. They thought a watch would be a helpful addition but they wouldn't reply on a watch to make any schedulings or communication.

04 Lo-fi Wireframes

Based on our sketches, our team compiled a set of lo-fi wireframes. These designs were desktop and mobile focused as these two sizes are most frequently used by the Leasing Agent or Renter.

We priotized desktop since they are most used while mobile serves as a synethsized version for "on-the-go" communication or schedulings.

We mapped out the functionalities for both sizes and investigated how to best represent them across the two.

The desktop view is broken down into dashboard, inbox, calendar/appointments, properties, tenants, and maintenance to echo our user interview findings.

The dashboard is a collection of all important information, accessible at a glance, while all other pages include more details in specific categories. We studied existing design patterns and incorportaed them into our design (i.e. listings, calendar views).

As for mobile, the menu on the left in desktop is condensed into a hamburger on the top left. The tab bar at the bottom includes what we evaluated to be the most important functions – inbox, calendar, and account.

It was challenging to think about how to organize information from the larger desktop screen to a small mobile screen while still displaying the same content. We asked the question "what would users most likely do with mobile vs. desktop?" to guide our design decisions.

Critique

Partnering with another team as well as discussion with our professors, we received valuable feedback on our lo-fi wireframes.

Notes

  • focus on a domain/problem we're solving
  • responsiveness –– how can we manage content for multiple stakeholders across multiple devices? (aiming for 4)
  • zero in on one specific scenario rather than the whole platform –– especially scheduling
  • interaction between leasing agent and renter into a specific scenario –– pain point of no-shows

Based on this feedback, we took a closer look and discussed the scenario we wanted to focus on and divided the different screens/flows amongst our team members. I was specifically responsible for the mobile apppointment screens.

05 Hi-fi Prototypes

Taking into account the feedback we received on our lo-fo wireframes, we continued with the design process towards hi-fi prototypes.

Initial Desktop Prototype

For the leasing agent, we kept the dashboard displaying weekly to-do's, inbox, and listings. It comprised of all the features we wanted to include but looking back we were definitely trying to make our design complete rather than novel.

As for the renter looking for an apartment and booking an appointment, our goal was to create a personalized, easy booking experience.

In addition to mobile, we also wanted to include a watch experience to increase the level of responsiveness in our design. Although the watch experience was overlooked in the speed dating session, our team felt like it still added value to the platform considering how time-sensitive notifications can be for the leasing agent.

Critique

Presenting our work, we received more valuable feedback mainly on our platform features. We realized we lacked novel features to differentiate our solution to existing ones. Additionally, we needed a more scenario based user flow to present with such that the audience is able to put themselves in the situation and understand the needs we've addresses.

Novel Feature Ideas

  • consolidated flow for appointment scheduling
  • adding "feedback flows" to integrate into the apartment matching experience
  • highlighting connections between the useful "data" the platform has –– i.e. similar properies, popular properties
Design Decisions

Since we designed for two stakeholders simultaneously and presented them together, we made a distingushment between the two using two contrasting colors. Using orange for the leasing agent and blue for the renters helped both us and our audience differentiate when looking at our designs.

Our UI follows a simplistic and modern design since from a UX aspect, we didn't want our design to distract or interfere from the user's goasl

Responsive Elements

We kept important components (cards, tags, and navbar) the same across the different devices.

There are also design patterns adopted from the digital product design space in order to make the platform easier to understand and use, ideally requiring no learning curve.

06 Final Design

Renter

The main change from the previous iteration is the "connect with agent" button on the left side. We found the previous "submit appointment" wording to be inaccurate here and added friction.

We also added a "selling point" tag next to some listening for users to easily learn more while browsing without needed to click into the listing.

We also changed the scheduling overlay design so that the user can select a specific time and receive feedback after submitting the apointment. Additionally, we received feedback during our pitch about the scheduling experience still feeling generic and not solving the issue of "dead-end" listings. Based on this, we also decided to add the listing agent to the listing to make the experience more personal from the beginning.

To address the common pain point of no-show tenants for viewings, we implemented two different solutions to ensure the renter has the opportunity to easily communicate with the leasing agent.

First, the renter is prompted to confirm the appointment 24 hours in advance to ensure timeliess of any changes needed on the renter's end.

Second, the platform pushes another notification 45 minutes prior to the appointment via inbox so that the renter is prompted to communicate with the leasing agent again for any clarifications or questions.

Viewings don't always end with the renter wanting to sign –– they're often interested other similar listings. Therefore, there's value in their feedback to improve the both the search process for the tenant and the listing or viewing process itself. Based on this, we thought it would be useful to incorporate a feedback form where the renter can provide their personal opinions and insight. The renter will also benefit from this since the data collected will allow the platform to generate better listing recommendations.

Leasing Agent

From the leasing agent's side, we added a detailed flow for sending documents. Originally, we had only one button that would send "all" documents but realized that was impractical and the leasing agent would need different document selections.

We also changed the appointment details screen for better precision on mobile tapping.

Lastly for the leasing agent dashboard, we added a new feature of "upcoming and current availabilites" that also utilizes "priority" tags for listings with longer vacant periods have higher visibilty. Since the dashboard was designed to improve workflow and efficiency, we felt adding small changes helped with visualizing priorities and minimize mental load.

Reflection

As the final assignment in Interaction Design Studio 1, I felt like everything we had spent the semester learning had built up to this point where we were challenged with creating our designs through a full process. I felt the prompt we received felt broad and difficult to address in the beginning but as we went through our initial research, scenarios & storyboards, and our first two design iterations, I finally understood the overall goal in building a solution to issues we identified across different devices for a whole responsive design. From there, I found it very helpful to narrow our scope, identify key innovative features, and define each piece in the larger platform and user flow.

While our deadlines were quick and required fast turnaround, as a team, we were able to work collaboratively and efficiently through good communication. I felt I learned a lot from working so closely with diverse skill sets and backgrounds. I especially found our in-class working sessions to be solid productive time for us to get feedback, discuss actions items, and decide on changes to implement. These sessions were crucial to our success as a team and allowed us to space and time needed toward such a complex project.
